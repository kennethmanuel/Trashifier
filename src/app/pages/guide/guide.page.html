SORTING AND RECYCLING GUIDE

One effective approach to tackle this issue is through household waste sorting, which follows the waste hierarchy.

source: United Nations Environmental Program (2013). Guidelines for National Waste Management Strategies Moving from Challenges to Opportunities

The waste hierarchy emphasizes prioritizing waste prevention, reduction, reuse, recycling, and only resorting to disposal as a last resort. In this article, we will explore the importance of household waste sorting based on the waste hierarchy and provide practical tips for implementing it effectively.

  1. The first step in the waste hierarchy is waste prevention. Households can minimize waste generation by adopting simple practices such as avoiding overconsumption, buying products with less packaging, and opting for durable and long-lasting items. By reducing unnecessary waste at its source, households can significantly contribute to sustainable waste management.

  2. If waste prevention is not feasible, the next step is waste reduction. Households can adopt strategies such as composting food scraps, which not only reduces waste sent to landfills but also provides nutrient-rich compost for gardening. Additionally, reducing paper and water usage, as well as reusing items like shopping bags, bottles, and containers, helps minimize waste production.

  3. Reusing items instead of disposing of them is an excellent way to extend their lifespan and reduce waste. Households can engage in practices like donating clothes, furniture, and electronics to local charities or organizing garage sales to give unwanted items a second life. Creative upcycling projects can also turn old or broken items into useful or decorative objects.

  4. Recycling plays a vital role in waste management. Households should segregate recyclable materials such as paper, cardboard, plastic, glass, and metal from general waste. Educating oneself about local recycling guidelines and participating in recycling programs and collection schemes are essential for effective recycling. By ensuring that recyclable materials are properly sorted, households contribute to the circular economy and conserve valuable resources.

  5. Disposal should be the last resort when all other options have been exhausted. Non-recyclable and non-compostable waste should be carefully disposed of according to local regulations. Households must follow proper waste disposal methods, such as using designated waste bins or taking waste to authorized disposal facilities. Reducing the volume of waste sent to landfills helps mitigate environmental pollution and preserves limited landfill space.
